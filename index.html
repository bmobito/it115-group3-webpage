Game Console Generations

In the video game industry, the market for home video game consoles has frequently been segmented into generations, grouping consoles that are considered to have shared in a competitive market place. Since the first home consoles in 1972, there have been nine defined home consoles generations.

A new console generation typically occurs approximately every five years, in keeping pace with Moore’s law for technology, though more recent generations have had extended periods due to the use of console revisions rather than completely new designs.

Consoles considered to be significant competition with one another are classed into generations. Systems such as handheld consoles are often omitted from these generations

Odyssey: Pioneering the Home Video Gaming Industry

In 1966, Ralph H. Baer, an engineer at Sanders Associates, pitched an idea for a device that could use a television to play games. After pitching the idea to multiple companies/sectors, TV manufacturer Magnavox picked it up.

In 1972 Magnavox released the Odyssey Video Game Unit into the US market. The Odyssey used analog circuitry (transistors and diodes) and was quite limited. It could display:

  • A vertical bar
  • 2 boxes whose position was controlled by each of the wired ‘controllers’
  • 1 box whose position was controlled by settings on the console

The games were simple and some required plastic overlays placed over the screen to give context similar to a game board. The Odyssey retailed for $100 and sold 69,000 units in the first year. Eventually 300,000 units were sold worldwide.

Odyssey pioneered many of the features that are common to gaming devices. One of those features was the pluggable modules to extend game functions. These modules are similar to cartridges in more modern consoles but modified the analog circuits rather than code for digital processors. Magnavox also released the Shooting Gallery accessory pack that included the first light gun gaming device and four shooting games.

Atari 2600

The second generation of gaming consoles was defined by inexpensive integrated circuits and swappable game cartridges which expanded the game libraries. Atari, a successful arcade game manufacturer created the Atari Video Computer System (rebranded Atari 2600) which retailed for $200. It became the most popular console in the second generation, selling over 30 million units.

In 1979, Atari’s four lead game developers left the company to found Activision and became the first third party computer game publisher. After Atari sued Activision for stealing trade secrets, Activision settled the lawsuit and agreed to pay royalties to publish games on the platform. Third party publishers follow this same business model today.

Sega Genesis

The fourth generation of game consoles was headlined by the Sega Genesis. It was the first console to truly adopt 16 bit graphics and was seen by the gaming community as a huge leap forward. It featured games such as Mortal Kombat and Sonic the Hedgehog.

Although Sega Genesis eventually failed as a console, the manufacturer Sega is remembered for pushing Nintendo, which initially had over 90% market share in the home console market at the time of the Genesis’s launch.

An enduring legacy of the Genesis is the Entertainment Software Rating Board (ESRB) stickers that are on every single game sold in the U.S. in response to the violent nature of some of their games.

Sony Playstation

The fifth generation of game consoles was led by the Sony Playstation. The Sony PlayStation was the brainchild of Ken Kutaragi, who was a Sony executive at the time.

Although the console started as a partnership between Sony and Nintendo, Nintendo abruptly cancelled the partnership and replaced Sony with Phillips a day after announcing the partnership. This infuriated the C.E.O. of Sony, Norio Ohga, and thus Sony Computer Entertainment (now known as Sony Interactive Entertainment) was formed by Ohga and Kutaragi to compete with Nintendo.

The PlayStation was released in 1995 and was a smash hit. It became the first console to sell over 100 million units. Hindsight is always 20/20 but being rejected by Nintendo became the best thing that ever happened to Sony.

Sixth generation gaming console

The sixth generation of video games was defined by the highest selling video game console of any kind to date, the PlayStation 2. If you compare the 155 million units sold with the 2 closest competitors of its generation (Nintendo GameCube: 22 million, Microsoft Xbox: 24 million) it is fair to say it blew away the competition. PS2 owes its success to many factors:

  1. At the time of its introduction the average cost of DVD players was 900$ and PS2 had DVD capability at $299. This gave even non gamers reason to purchase the console.
  2. It was the first console to allow backwards compatibility and allowed PS1 gamers (and its over 100 million console owners) to play their PS1 games on the PS2.
  3. The console had a broad assortment of game developers pledging to produce games for the system.

All of these factors contributed to the most successful console to date.

Seventh generation gaming console

The seventh generation of game consoles began on November 22, 2005 with the release of Microsoft’s Xbox 360 home console. This was followed by the release of Sony Computer Entertainment’s PlayStation 3 on November 17, 2006 and Nintendo’s Wii on November 19, 2006. The seventh generation slowly began to wind down when Nintendo began cutting back on Wii production in the early 2010s.

Not all home consoles are defined as part of this generation.

History of handheld consoles

Milton Bradley Microvision (1979) - World's first handheld console released by Milton Bradley. Because the screen of this console was so small it was named the name Microvision. Jay Smith used Vectrex system to design this handheld console.

GameBoy (1989) - Released by Nintendo, and designed by Gunpei Yokoi. This gaming device can stay powered for about 20 hours of gameplay.

Lynx (1989) - The first handheld console with a color LCD display. However, battery of this device was not as strong as the GamBoy; it was eventually abandoned.

Game.com (1997) - Tiger Electronics released The Game.com. It was the first touchscreen handheld console that was internet-enabled.

GameBoy Advance (GBA) (2001) - The stronger version of Gameboy. Up to four players can share a cartridge with a network available for multiplayer.

GameBoy Advance Special (GBA-SP) (2003) - GBA-SP is smaller and has a flip screen so it's easier for players to carry. The screen also lights up so users can play in the dark.

Nintendo Dual Screen (NDS) (2004) - The most popular handheld console in history--154.02 million units have been sold since its release. This console can be fliped and has a touch screen. NDS can use WiFi to make connections between 16 players gaming together. NDS is also compatible with all GBA games.

PlayStation Portable (2004) - Considered the first handheld video game system to use an optical disc format (Universal Media Disc).

Nintendo Switch (2017) - A powerful console that can be used as handheld. Joy-Con is the special wireless controller of Switch.
